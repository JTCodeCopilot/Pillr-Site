Diagnosis guide

How is ADHD diagnosed?

There is usually not one single test. A proper assessment often includes your history, your current struggles, and a check for other causes that can look similar.

Download Pillr More guides

What an assessment often includes

A clinician will usually ask about long-term patterns, not just what happened this week. They may ask about school, work, home life, memory, focus, planning, emotional ups and downs, sleep, and daily function.

  • Questions about current symptoms and how they affect your life
  • Questions about childhood patterns or earlier years
  • A review of other possible causes, like anxiety, sleep issues, or burnout
  • Sometimes forms for you or someone who knows you well

What to bring

The most useful thing is examples. Bring a short list of recent situations where focus, memory, planning, timing, or routine caused problems.

If medication is part of the picture

Bring your current medication list and note any missed doses, timing issues, side effects, or changes you have noticed. Clear records can make the conversation more useful.

Preparation tip

If you already use Pillr, bring your dose history or exported records. Concrete examples are easier to work with than trying to remember everything in the room.

Good questions to ask

  • What else could explain these symptoms?
  • What parts of my history matter most?
  • If ADHD is likely, what are the next steps?
  • How should I track symptoms or medication between visits?

What this means

A diagnosis is about patterns and impact, not perfection. You do not need to explain everything perfectly. You do need clear examples.
