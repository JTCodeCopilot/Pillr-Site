Speak with your doctor, psychiatrist, pharmacist, or prescribing clinician about medication timing, missed doses, side effects, or treatment decisions.

Why side effect notes are easy to lose

The National Institute of Mental Health describes ADHD as affecting attention, organisation, and daily functioning in adults. That matters because side effect notes often depend on exactly the skills ADHD can make harder: noticing, remembering, and explaining a pattern later.

A single rough day may not tell you much. A few short notes across similar days can be more useful, especially before a medication review.

Track the pattern, not every feeling

Side effect tracking does not need to become a long health diary. Start with the smallest useful record:

What to record Plain example
Dose timing "Taken at 8:10, later than usual."
Dose status "Taken", "skipped", "late", or "not sure".
Side effect note "Low appetite at lunch" or "headache after late lunch".
Routine context "Poor sleep", "extra coffee", "stressful meeting", or "missed breakfast".
Question to ask "Does this pattern matter for my medication review?"

Use simple language. "Headache after late lunch" or "felt wired after coffee and dose" is often enough to start a better conversation.

Separate side effects from routine noise

Adults with ADHD often have busy, uneven days. Poor sleep, missed meals, a stressful meeting, dehydration, caffeine, or a late dose can all make a day feel different.

The point is not to decide the cause yourself. The point is to avoid walking into an appointment with only a vague memory of "something felt off." A record lets you ask, "Does this pattern matter?"

If a symptom feels severe, sudden, unsafe, or unusual for you, do not wait for a perfect tracking pattern. Contact a clinician, pharmacist, local urgent care service, or emergency service as appropriate.

Bring clearer notes to review

NICE guidance recommends monitoring ADHD medication effectiveness and adverse effects, and encouraging people taking ADHD medication to record adverse effects. It also includes monitoring such as weight, heart rate, and blood pressure at review points.

Useful questions might be:

  • "Which side effects should I track more closely?"
  • "Could this be related to dose timing, sleep, food, caffeine, or another medicine?"
  • "When should I contact you sooner instead of waiting?"
  • "What information would help at our next review?"

Side effect note checklist

  • Record dose time
  • Note sleep and food
  • Track mood and focus
  • Write down new symptoms
  • Add a question to ask
  • Bring patterns to review

A short note you can keep is better than a detailed diary that disappears.

Keep notes plain

You do not need clinical language. Use words you would actually say in an appointment.

Look for repeats

A repeated pattern is usually more useful than trying to explain one difficult day perfectly.

What should an ADHD medication side effect tracker include?

Ask your clinician or pharmacist what matters for your medication. Common useful notes include dose time, sleep, appetite, mood, energy, focus, physical symptoms, and anything new or worrying.

How often should I write side effect notes?

Keep it realistic. A short note when something changes, or a quick daily check-in during a review period, is usually more useful than a detailed diary you cannot keep.

Should I stop or change medication if I notice side effects?

Do not change medication based on a general article or tracking note alone. Contact your clinician or pharmacist for advice that fits your prescription and health history.

Can Pillr identify medication side effects?

No. Pillr helps you record dose timing, notes, and routines, but it does not identify side effects, diagnose problems, or replace medical advice.
