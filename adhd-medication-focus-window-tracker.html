What a focus window tracker is for

A focus window tracker connects an actual dose record with a few short observations from the same day. It can help you notice repeated patterns without asking you to remember the whole week later.

The National Institute of Mental Health notes that adults with ADHD may find it difficult to stay organised, keep appointments, and complete daily tasks. A small external record can reduce how much of the routine you need to hold in memory.

A tracking window is not proof that medication started or stopped working at an exact time. It does not measure medication levels, diagnose symptoms, or tell you when to take another dose.

Record five small things

Use the same fields each day. Short, repeatable notes are easier to compare than a long diary written only when something goes wrong.

What to record Simple prompt Example
Dose record What happened and when? "Taken at 8:10 AM"
First check-in What felt easier or harder? "Started report without restarting"
Later change What changed, and around when? "More distractible after 2 PM"
Daily context What else may matter? "Poor sleep, late lunch, busy office"
Review question What needs a professional answer? "What should I track if this repeats?"

If you notice a side effect, record it plainly rather than trying to interpret it. Ask a clinician or pharmacist about anything concerning, new, severe, or persistent.

Compare several days, not one moment

One difficult afternoon may reflect medication timing, sleep, food, caffeine, stress, workload, or something else. A tracker is most useful when it keeps those possibilities visible instead of deciding on a cause.

  1. Log the actual dose time and status.
  2. Choose one or two brief check-in moments you can repeat.
  3. Use plain descriptions such as "settled", "scattered", or "headache".
  4. Add only the daily context that may help explain a difference.
  5. After a week, mark patterns that appeared more than once.

NICE ADHD guidance recommends monitoring medication effectiveness and adverse effects and documenting them in the person's notes. Your tracker can support that conversation, but treatment interpretation belongs with the professional managing your care.

Keep estimates separate from observations

A timeline may show an estimated window based on settings. Your notes show what you observed. Keep both, but do not treat them as the same thing.

  • Estimate: a window calculated from timing values you use.
  • Observation: what you noticed at a real time on a real day.
  • Clinical decision: an answer about medication, timing, side effects, or treatment from your clinician or pharmacist.

This separation makes the record more honest. It also prevents a neat-looking timeline from being mistaken for medical certainty.

Turn the pattern into appointment questions

Before an appointment, reduce the week to a short summary:

  • What pattern repeated?
  • Which days were different?
  • What side effects or concerns did you record?
  • What medication or timing question needs an answer?

Bring the original dose times as well as the summary. Do not change your dose or schedule because a timeline looks early, late, short, or long.

Keep the boundary clear

What is an ADHD medication focus window?

A focus window is a personal tracking period linked to a logged dose and your own timing notes. It can help organise observations, but it is not a measurement of medication levels or effectiveness.

Can an app tell exactly when ADHD medication kicks in or wears off?

No. An app can show an estimate based on timing settings and dose records, but it cannot measure what medication is doing in your body. Discuss timing or treatment questions with your clinician or pharmacist.

What should I record in a focus window tracker?

Record the actual dose time, taken or skipped status, brief focus or side-effect notes, useful daily context, and any question you want to raise at an appointment.

How does Pillr's Focus Timeline work?

For supported stimulant medication, a logged dose can create an estimated Focus Timeline from your own onset and duration settings. It is a tracking and planning aid, not medical certainty.

Should I change medication timing based on a focus timeline?

No. Do not change medication timing or dose based on a timeline or general article. Ask your doctor, psychiatrist, pharmacist, or prescribing clinician what is appropriate for you.
