Adult ADHD medication guide

ADHD medication review
appointment guide for adults

Medication reviews are easier when you do not have to remember everything on the spot.

A short dose history, a few routine notes, and clear questions can help you have a calmer, more useful conversation with your clinician.

This guide is general information only. Pillr helps with tracking and routines, but it does not replace medical advice. For questions about medication changes, missed doses, side effects, or treatment, speak with your clinician or pharmacist.

Why preparation matters

Adults with ADHD often walk into appointments with a strong feeling that something has been working, not working, or changing, but the details can be hard to recall under pressure.

Health sources such as the National Institute of Mental Health describe ADHD as affecting organisation, follow-through, work, relationships, and daily life. That is exactly why a written record can help. It moves the appointment from “I think” to “here is what happened.”

NICE guidance also includes medication monitoring, review, and treatment adherence as part of ADHD care. You do not need perfect notes. You need useful ones.

Track the few facts that matter most

Do not try to create a huge report. A small, honest record is usually easier to keep and easier to read.

Before a medication review, it can help to note:

  • What medication you take and when you usually take it
  • How often doses were taken, skipped, late, or missed
  • Any days when timing clearly affected sleep, appetite, focus, or mood
  • Refill delays, supply issues, or pharmacy problems
  • Questions you keep meaning to ask, but forget during appointments

Keep the notes factual. You are not trying to diagnose yourself or prove anything. You are giving your clinician a clearer starting point.

Write down patterns, not every thought

A medication review does not need a diary of every day. Look for repeated patterns instead.

Useful examples sound like this:

  • “I missed three morning doses in the last two weeks.”
  • “I often remember the dose after my first meeting, not before.”
  • “I noticed sleep was harder on days I took medication later than usual.”
  • “I ran low before I realised I needed a refill.”
  • “I am not sure what to do when I notice a missed dose late in the day.”

These are practical details your clinician or pharmacist can respond to. They are also easier to discuss than a vague memory that the month felt messy.

Separate routine problems from medical questions

Some problems are about the system around the medication. For example, the reminder comes at a bad time, the bottle is not where you take breakfast, or you forget to refill until supply is low.

Other questions are medical. Dose timing, side effects, missed-dose instructions, medication changes, and whether a medicine is right for you should come from your clinician or pharmacist, not from a checklist or app.

Use your notes to make that split clearer: “This is a routine problem I can improve” versus “This is a question I need to ask.”

Appointment checklist

  • Current medication list
  • Taken, skipped, late, and missed doses
  • Sleep, appetite, focus, or mood patterns
  • Refill or supply issues
  • Questions for your clinician

A clear record can make the appointment feel less rushed and more useful.

Bring facts

A short dose history is easier to discuss than trying to remember the whole month.

Ask clearly

Write the medical questions down before the appointment so they do not disappear in the moment.

What should I bring to an ADHD medication review appointment?

Bring your current medication list, dose history, missed or skipped dose notes, side effect notes, refill issues, and the questions you want answered.

How far back should my ADHD medication notes go?

A few recent weeks of clear notes can be useful, but follow your clinician's guidance if they ask for a specific time range.

Should I change my dose before a medication review?

Do not change your dose based on a general guide. Follow your prescription instructions and ask your clinician or pharmacist.
