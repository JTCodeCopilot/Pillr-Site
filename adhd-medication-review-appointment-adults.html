Adult ADHD medication guide

ADHD medication
appointment notes for adults

Medication appointments are easier when you do not have to rebuild the last few weeks from memory.

A short dose history, clear questions, and a simple follow-up record can make the conversation calmer and more useful.

Updated July 18, 2026

Back to home

This guide is for general information only and is not medical advice. Pillr helps with medication reminders, dose tracking, and routine notes, but it does not diagnose, treat, prevent, or cure ADHD. Speak with your doctor, psychiatrist, pharmacist, or prescribing clinician about medication timing, missed doses, side effects, or treatment decisions.

Start with the appointment question

Adults with ADHD often know something changed, but the details can be hard to recall under pressure. You might remember that mornings felt messy, focus faded earlier, sleep changed, or refills became stressful, but not exactly when or how often.

Health sources such as the National Institute of Mental Health describe ADHD as involving patterns like difficulty staying organised, keeping on task, and getting things done across daily life. That is why a short written record can help. It moves the appointment from "I think" to "here is what happened."

Start with one question you want answered. For example: "What should I do when I notice a missed dose late?" or "Which side effects should I track more closely?" The notes should support that question, not become homework for its own sake.

Use a simple appointment notes template

You do not need a perfect diary. A small, factual record is usually easier to keep and easier to read.

What to note Plain example
Dose history "Taken most mornings, skipped twice, unsure once."
Timing pattern "Often remembered after first meeting, not before."
Side effects or symptoms "Sleep felt harder on three late-dose days."
Refill or supply issue "Ran low before I realised I needed a refill."
Question to ask "What should I do if this pattern repeats?"

Keep the notes factual. You are not trying to diagnose yourself or decide a medication change. You are giving your clinician or pharmacist a clearer starting point.

Separate routine problems from medical questions

Some problems are about the system around the medication. For example, the reminder comes at a bad time, the bottle is not where you usually start the day, or refill planning stays invisible until supply is low.

Other questions are medical. Dose timing, side effects, missed-dose instructions, medication changes, and whether a medicine is right for you should come from your doctor, psychiatrist, pharmacist, or prescribing clinician.

NICE ADHD guidance includes medication monitoring, treatment adherence, adverse effects, and the effects of missed doses as review topics. That is a useful reminder that routine friction is worth raising, not hiding.

Keep the final list short

Before the appointment, turn the notes into a short list you can actually use in the room:

  • Current medication and usual dose time
  • Recent taken, skipped, missed, late, or uncertain doses
  • Any repeated sleep, appetite, focus, mood, or side effect pattern
  • Refill delays, low supply, or pharmacy problems
  • The top two or three questions you want answered

If something feels severe, sudden, unsafe, or unusual for you, do not wait for the next routine appointment. Contact a qualified professional, local urgent care service, or emergency service as appropriate.

Use a before, during, and after workflow

A small three-part plan can keep the appointment useful without turning it into another large project:

  1. Before: save the appointment time, clinician, medications to review, current symptoms or side effects, and your top two or three questions.
  2. During: use the short question list. If you record or transcribe the visit, ask permission from everyone involved and check the result rather than treating it as a perfect record.
  3. After: review the summary, keep only confirmed next steps, and add any follow-up task or reminder you agreed on.

Appointment notes should never change a medication, dose, schedule, or reminder automatically. Confirm medication changes with your clinician and update your routine only from their instructions.

What should I write down before an ADHD medication appointment?

Write down your current medication list, recent dose history, missed or skipped dose notes, side effect notes, refill issues, and the questions you want answered.

How far back should ADHD medication notes go?

A few recent weeks of clear notes can be useful, but follow your clinician's guidance if they ask for a specific time range.

Should I change my dose before a medication review?

Do not change your dose based on a general guide. Follow your prescription instructions and ask your clinician or pharmacist.

Can Pillr help me prepare appointment notes?

Pillr can keep dose history, notes, and questions together, and My Appointment can save visit details and an editable summary on iPhone. It does not replace medical advice.

Can Pillr record and summarise an appointment?
